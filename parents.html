Spotting the Red Flags

Unfortunately the majority of children who are about to be, or have been victimized never tell anything on their own. It's up to us to spot the warning signs.

  • Only one in five kids who have been sexually abused report it.
  • If your child tells you that he doesn't want to be around a particular person, take them seriously.
  • Some children may show physical signs such as unexplained urinary infections, redness, or swelling in the genital area.
  • If you suspect that your child has been abused, the most important thing is to NOT investigate by yourself! Call the Wolf Watch hotline for anomynous professional advice, or contact your local child-protection agency.

How Can I Protect My Child From Sexual Assault?

All children are at risk of sexual abuse, regardless of race or gender. Neither is child sexual abuse uncommon. One in four girls and one in six boys will be sexually abused before they turn 18, according to the NationalSexual Violence Resource Center. Be proactive in keeping your children safe.

  • Behavioral signs: Example, shrinking away from or seeming threatened by physical contact.
  • Get to know the people in your child’s life.
  • Choose caregivers carefully, be diligent about screening caregivers for your child.
  • Encourage children to speak up. Let them know they won't get in trouble and be available to talk to.
  • Teach your child about boundaries. Let your child know other people don't have the right to touch them or make them uncomfortable.
